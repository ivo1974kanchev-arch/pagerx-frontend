📋 How The Analysis Works

Every page was run through PageRX, which evaluates landing pages across 6 categories:

  • META Metadata & SEO — title tags, meta descriptions, OG/Twitter cards, structured data
  • CONTENT Content & Copy — value proposition clarity, headline strength, readability, social proof
  • STRUCTURE Structure & UX — heading hierarchy, navigation, mobile responsiveness, page flow
  • TRUST Trust & Credibility — testimonials, security badges, privacy signals, HTTPS enforcement
  • CONVERSION Conversion Design — CTA placement and copy, form length, friction points, urgency signals
  • TECHNICAL Technical Quality — page speed, semantic HTML, JS dependencies, CSP headers, accessibility

Each category generates a sub-score out of 100. The overall score is the average across all six. The tool also provides a deep 5-perspective AI analysis for every page.

⚡ Key finding: The average score across all 20 pages was 72/100. The lowest was 38, the highest was 91. Most indie hackers are leaving 28 points on the table — and those points directly correlate with conversion rate.

📊 Common Patterns Among Show HN Landing Pages

Across all 20 pages, these patterns emerged consistently:

  • 🔥 Generic hero headlines (14/20 pages) — Vague taglines like "Simplify Your Workflow." The top 20% used specific, benefit-driven copy with a concrete use case.
  • 📱 Mobile as an afterthought (12/20 pages) — Fixed-width containers, tiny tap targets, horizontal scrolling on phones. Built on a Mac, never tested on mobile.
  • 🧩 Missing semantic HTML (11/20 pages) — No <nav>, no <main>, no heading hierarchy. Hurts both SEO and accessibility.
  • 🏷️ No trust signals (10/20 pages) — Zero testimonials, no user count, no privacy policy. On HN's skeptical audience, this is a conversion killer.
  • ⚡ Bloated pages with unnecessary JS (9/20 pages) — Landing pages loading 15+ scripts for what could be a 150KB static page. Every script is a speed tax.
  • 📋 Missing structured data (15/20 pages) — Almost no pages had JSON-LD or schema.org markup. A missed SEO opportunity for launch-day traction.

💡 The common thread: Most indie hackers optimize for building the product and forget that the landing page is the product for the first 10 seconds of a visitor's experience. The gap between a 46 and an 89 isn't months of work — it's a focused weekend of fixing the 6 things PageRX flags.

🚨 The 3 Biggest Mistakes Indie Hackers Make

❌ Mistake #1: Writing for Themselves, Not Their Audience

The single most common pattern was feature-first, benefit-nowhere copy. Indie hackers describe what their product does ("AI-powered bookmarking with NLP tagging") instead of what it does for the user. PageRX's Content analysis caught this on 80% of pages. The fix: after every feature sentence, add "...which means [benefit]." If you can't, rewrite it.

❌ Mistake #2: Shipping a Desktop-Only Experience on a Mobile-First World

Over half the pages were effectively broken on a phone — buttons overlapping, text overflowing, navigation impossible. The HN audience is predominantly mobile. A desktop-only page tells visitors: "I didn't care enough to test this on your device." PageRX flags mobile responsiveness in the Structure category. Fixing it typically means 4-6 CSS media queries and changing `width: 300px` to `max-width: 100%`.

❌ Mistake #3: No Social Proof on a Platform Built on Social Proof

HN is the most social-proof-driven audience on the internet. Yet 50% of indie hacker landing pages have zero social proof. No testimonials, no user count, no "as seen on" badges. If you're launching on Show HN, your landing page should have beta user testimonials, a GitHub star count, or a "Join 500+ users" counter. PageRX's Trust category caught this gap on every sub-70 scorer.

🔧 What The Top 10% Did Differently

The 2 highest-scoring pages (91 and 90) shared a specific set of traits that the rest didn't:

  • One sentence that explains the product to a non-technical person. Not "AI-powered multi-modal vector database" but "Find any file you've ever saved in under 2 seconds."
  • A single, unambiguous primary CTA. One button, one action, one reason to click it.
  • Full structured data across 3 schema types — Both had Article, Product, and BreadcrumbList JSON-LD. A meaningful SEO edge for long-tail keywords.
  • Sub-1-second load time — Under 900ms on 4G. No heavy frameworks, just optimized HTML, one CSS file, and one web font.
  • Social proof above the fold — "4.9/5 from 128 reviews" and "Used by teams at [recognizable name]" appeared before scrolling.

The top 10% didn't do anything clever. They did the basics perfectly. And the basics — clear value prop, fast load, mobile responsive, social proof, one CTA — are exactly what PageRX checks in under 60 seconds.
